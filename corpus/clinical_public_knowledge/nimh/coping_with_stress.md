# Coping with Everyday Stress

Stress is the physical or mental response to an external cause — a heavy
workload, a difficult conversation, a deadline, illness, a loss. It is a
normal part of life. Short bursts of stress can be useful, helping a
person stay alert and focused. Chronic stress — stress that persists over
a long period — is different. Over time, chronic stress can wear down the
body and contribute to physical and mental health problems.

## How stress shows up

Stress affects people differently. Common signs include:

- Sleep changes — trouble falling asleep, waking through the night,
  sleeping much more than usual.
- Appetite changes — eating more, eating less, eating less mindfully.
- Headaches, muscle tension (jaw, shoulders, back), digestive issues.
- Difficulty concentrating; small decisions feeling unusually heavy.
- Irritability with people who do not deserve it.
- A short fuse for situations that did not previously bother the person.
- Feelings of being overwhelmed or "frozen."
- Increased use of alcohol, nicotine, or other substances.

## Strategies that help

No single technique works for everyone. The point is to have a few
options on hand and to use them before stress accumulates, not after.

- **Move regularly.** Even short walks help. Movement is one of the
  best-studied interventions for stress.
- **Sleep.** Consistent sleep timing matters as much as duration. Going
  to bed and waking at similar times stabilizes mood and energy.
- **Stay connected.** Reach out to a friend, family member, or community.
  Isolation amplifies stress; connection reduces it.
- **Notice substance use.** Alcohol, nicotine, and caffeine can all
  feel like stress relief in the moment but increase baseline stress
  over time.
- **Take breaks.** Step away from news, social media, and screens
  intentionally. Five minutes counts.
- **Set small, realistic goals.** "Finish the project" is not a goal;
  "spend 15 minutes on the next section" is.
- **Practice a simple grounding technique.** Slow breathing, naming five
  things in the room, brief mindfulness — these are not magic, but they
  interrupt the stress response enough to think more clearly.

## When stress becomes more

If stress lasts longer than a few weeks, gets in the way of work, school,
or relationships, or is paired with persistent sadness, hopelessness, or
worry, it is worth talking to a health care provider. Stress and
depression and anxiety can overlap; sorting them out is what professionals
are for.

## Crisis resources

If a person is in crisis or having thoughts of suicide or self-harm, the
988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (United States) is available 24/7 by
call or text.

## Important note

This is general guidance. It is not a substitute for professional advice
when stress is severe, persistent, or accompanied by other symptoms.
