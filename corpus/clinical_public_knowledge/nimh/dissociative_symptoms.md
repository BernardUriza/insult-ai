# Dissociative Symptoms — Awareness

Dissociation is the experience of feeling disconnected from
thoughts, feelings, memories, surroundings, actions, or identity.
A brief dissociative experience — zoning out on a long drive,
losing track of time during an immersive task — is common and not
itself a problem. The clinical threshold is more severe,
distressing, persistent, and disruptive.

This document is psychoeducation only. The Roast Coach is not a
diagnostic tool for dissociative conditions; if a user describes
recurring, distressing dissociation, the appropriate move is to
encourage talking to a mental health professional.

## Common signs to be aware of

- **Depersonalization:** feeling detached from one's own body,
  thoughts, or emotions; "watching myself from outside."
- **Derealization:** feeling that the surrounding world is unreal,
  foggy, dreamlike, visually distorted, or distant.
- **Gaps in memory:** missing chunks of time, especially around
  stressful events, that go beyond ordinary forgetfulness.
- **A sense of disconnection** from one's own life, name, history,
  or sense of self.

## When to consider talking to someone

- If dissociative experiences are frequent, distressing, or
  interfering with work or relationships.
- If they are paired with a history of trauma.
- If they happen in the context of intense emotional pain or
  self-harm.

## Connection to trauma

Many dissociative experiences develop as the mind's way of coping
with overwhelming stress, often during traumatic events. This makes
sense as a protective response in the short term and can become
its own pattern over time. Trauma-focused psychotherapy is the
primary treatment approach for dissociative symptoms tied to
trauma.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation, not a diagnostic tool. Dissociative
disorders are diagnosed by qualified clinicians using structured
assessments. Self-diagnosis from internet checklists is not
reliable; the symptoms overlap with other conditions in ways that
require trained evaluation.
