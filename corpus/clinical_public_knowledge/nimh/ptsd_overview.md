# Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder — Overview

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) is a condition that can develop
in some people who have experienced a shocking, scary, or dangerous
event. Most people who go through a traumatic event have some
short-term difficulty adjusting and coping; with time and self-care,
many recover. For some, symptoms persist for months or years and
interfere with daily life; that pattern can be PTSD.

## Events that can trigger PTSD

- Combat or war exposure.
- Serious accidents.
- Natural disasters.
- Physical, sexual, or other interpersonal violence — including events
  experienced second-hand by close family members.
- Sudden, unexpected death of a loved one.
- Other life-threatening or deeply distressing events.

PTSD is not a sign of weakness. It is a recognized response, and many
factors influence whether a given person develops it.

## Categories of symptoms

PTSD symptoms typically begin within three months of the event but can
also surface later. They are commonly grouped into four categories:

### Re-experiencing symptoms

- Flashbacks — reliving the event, sometimes with physical symptoms
  like a racing heart or sweating.
- Recurrent, distressing memories.
- Recurrent nightmares related to the event.
- Frightening thoughts.

### Avoidance symptoms

- Staying away from places, events, or objects that recall the
  experience.
- Avoiding thoughts or feelings related to the event.

### Arousal and reactivity symptoms

- Being easily startled.
- Feeling tense, "on guard," or on edge.
- Difficulty sleeping.
- Angry outbursts.

### Mood and cognition symptoms

- Trouble remembering key features of the event.
- Negative thoughts about oneself or the world.
- Distorted feelings of guilt or blame.
- Loss of interest in activities once enjoyed.

For a PTSD diagnosis, an adult must have symptoms from each category
for at least a month, and the symptoms must affect daily life.

## When to seek help

If symptoms have lasted longer than a month, are getting in the way of
work, school, or relationships, or are paired with substance use or
thoughts of self-harm, it is time to reach out to a health care
provider. Earlier treatment tends to work better, but treatment is not
"too late" at any point.

## What treatment can look like

PTSD is treatable. The two main approaches are psychotherapy
(particularly trauma-focused therapies that have strong research
support) and medication, often used together. The right plan depends
on the person, their history, and their preferences. A skilled
clinician walks the person through what each approach looks like
before deciding.

If a person is in crisis or having thoughts of suicide or self-harm,
the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (United States) is available
24/7 by call or text.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation, not a diagnostic tool. PTSD is
diagnosed by qualified clinicians, not by checklists on the internet.
