# Acute Stress and the First Days After a Trauma

Not every trauma reaction is PTSD. Most people exposed to a deeply
distressing or terrifying event experience some short-term reaction;
many of those reactions resolve in days to weeks. Acute stress
disorder (ASD) is the term used when significant symptoms appear in
the first month after a traumatic event and last at least three
days. PTSD is the term when symptoms persist beyond a month.

This document is for the early window — what is common to feel,
what helps, and when to seek help. It pairs with the PTSD overview
for the longer-term arc.

## What is common in the first days

- Recurring, intrusive memories of the event.
- Nightmares.
- Flashbacks — brief reliving of the event.
- Avoidance of reminders.
- Emotional numbness, feeling "shut down."
- Hypervigilance — feeling on edge, easily startled.
- Difficulty sleeping or concentrating.
- Intense feelings of guilt, shame, sadness, or anger.
- Physical symptoms: rapid heartbeat, sweating, headache, nausea.

These reactions, on their own, do not mean a person will develop
PTSD. They are signs the nervous system is processing the event.

## What helps in the early window

- **Safety first.** If the event is still ongoing or there is
  continued danger, getting to safety is the priority.
- **Basic care.** Eating, drinking water, sleeping when possible.
  Trauma reactions deplete physical reserves quickly.
- **Stay connected.** Time alone has its place; complete isolation
  in the first days tends to worsen symptoms.
- **Talk if and when ready.** Talking can help, but forced talking
  ("debriefing" in the first 24–48 hours) does not. Pace it to
  what feels manageable.
- **Avoid heavy alcohol or other substances.** They mute symptoms
  short-term and tend to worsen recovery.
- **Be patient with the body.** Symptoms come in waves; a bad hour
  does not mean a permanent state.

## When to reach out for professional help

- If symptoms are severe.
- If symptoms are not easing after two to four weeks.
- If safety concerns — thoughts of self-harm or suicide, intense
  dissociation — are present.
- If functioning is significantly impaired (cannot work, cannot
  care for self or dependents).

Trauma-focused therapies and, in some cases, brief interventions in
the acute window can help reduce the likelihood of longer-term
PTSD. Earlier engagement usually produces better outcomes than
waiting.

## When it is an emergency

If a person is in immediate crisis or having thoughts of suicide
or self-harm, that is an emergency. In the US, the 988 Suicide and
Crisis Lifeline is available 24/7 by call or text.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation, not a diagnostic tool. ASD and
PTSD are diagnosed by qualified clinicians.
