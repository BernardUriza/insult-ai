# Seasonal Affective Disorder — Overview

Seasonal affective disorder (SAD) is a type of depression that comes
and goes with the seasons. The most common pattern is symptoms in the
late fall and winter, with relief in the spring and summer — what
people sometimes call "winter blues" at a more severe level. A less
common pattern is the reverse, with summer depression and winter
relief.

SAD is not just feeling tired during shorter days. The clinical
threshold is symptoms that meaningfully affect mood, energy, sleep,
and daily function, and that recur in a clear seasonal pattern.

## Common signs (winter pattern)

- Persistent sad, low, or "flat" mood.
- Loss of interest in activities normally enjoyed.
- Low energy; feeling sluggish.
- Sleeping more than usual but not feeling rested.
- Increased appetite, especially for carbohydrates; weight gain.
- Difficulty concentrating.
- Withdrawing socially; "hibernating."

## Common signs (summer pattern, less common)

- Trouble sleeping (insomnia).
- Poor appetite; weight loss.
- Restlessness and agitation.
- Anxiety.

## When to seek help

If symptoms recur for two or more years in roughly the same season,
last for weeks at a time, and interfere with work, relationships, or
daily life, it is worth talking to a health care provider before the
next predicted onset. Catching SAD before it deepens tends to make
treatment more effective.

## What treatment can look like

SAD responds to several approaches, often combined:

- **Light therapy** — daily exposure to a bright light box of the
  appropriate intensity, typically used in the morning. Best-studied
  intervention for winter-pattern SAD.
- **Psychotherapy** — cognitive behavioral therapy adapted for SAD.
- **Medication** — antidepressants, often started in advance of the
  symptomatic season.
- **Vitamin D** — sometimes recommended when blood levels are low.

Sleep timing, regular morning movement outdoors, and reducing alcohol
use are reasonable complements at any tier.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation, not a diagnostic tool. SAD is
diagnosed by qualified clinicians.
