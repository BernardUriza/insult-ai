# Eating Disorders — Overview

Eating disorders are serious medical illnesses marked by severe
disturbances in eating behaviors and related thoughts and emotions.
They are not lifestyle choices. They affect people of any age,
gender, race, and body size, and they have one of the highest
mortality rates of any mental illness when untreated.

## Common types

- **Anorexia nervosa:** severe food restriction, intense fear of
  gaining weight, distorted body image. Some people purge. Weight may
  be visibly low or in a "normal" range — the latter is sometimes
  called atypical anorexia, equally serious.
- **Bulimia nervosa:** recurring episodes of eating large amounts in
  a short period (binges), followed by behaviors meant to prevent
  weight gain — vomiting, laxative use, fasting, excessive exercise.
- **Binge-eating disorder:** recurring binge episodes without the
  compensatory behaviors of bulimia. Often accompanied by shame and
  distress.
- **Avoidant/restrictive food intake disorder (ARFID):** limited
  intake based on lack of interest, sensory aversion, or fear of
  aversive consequences, rather than body image concerns.

## Common signs

- Preoccupation with food, weight, calories, or body image.
- Significant changes in eating patterns — restricting, binging,
  hiding food, eating in secret.
- Physical signs: dental issues, frequent stomach problems, dizziness,
  changes in menstrual cycle, fatigue, brittle hair or nails.
- Social withdrawal around meals.
- Rigid food rules ("good" / "bad" food) that drive distress.
- Excessive exercise or "compensating" patterns.

## When to seek help

Eating disorders are most treatable when caught early, but it is
never "too late." If the user, or someone they know, is restricting
food, binging, purging, exercising compulsively, or thinking about
food/weight in a way that interferes with daily life, it is worth
reaching out to a clinician with eating-disorder training. The user's
primary care provider can usually make a referral.

## What treatment can look like

Treatment is typically multi-modal: psychotherapy specific to eating
disorders (family-based treatment, CBT-E, and others), nutrition
support from a registered dietitian familiar with eating disorders,
medical monitoring, and sometimes medication for co-occurring
conditions. Higher levels of care (intensive outpatient, partial
hospitalization, inpatient) exist for medical or psychiatric severity.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation. Eating disorders are serious medical
illnesses; diagnosis and treatment require a qualified clinician.
