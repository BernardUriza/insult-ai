# Mental Illness — How Common Is It?

Mental illnesses are common in the United States. Per NIMH summaries
of national surveys, nearly one in five U.S. adults lives with a
mental illness in any given year. The figures shift slightly year to
year and across surveys, but the headline is stable: mental illness
is common, and the person who is struggling is not alone.

This document exists so the Roast Coach has retrievable, sourced
text it can lean on when the user expresses isolation ("I'm the only
one who feels this way" / "everyone else has their stuff together").
The factual answer is that this is a very ordinary human experience.

## Headline figures (NIMH summaries)

- Roughly 1 in 5 U.S. adults lives with a mental illness in a given
  year ("Any Mental Illness," AMI).
- A smaller subset (roughly 1 in 25 adults) lives with a serious
  mental illness in a given year ("Serious Mental Illness," SMI).
- Prevalence varies by condition. Anxiety disorders are among the
  most common. Major depressive episode prevalence among adults is
  in the high single digits per year.
- Many people with mental illness do not receive treatment in any
  given year — the treatment gap is sizable.

Exact percentages drift across the most recent surveys and are
updated by NIMH; the orders of magnitude above are robust.

## What "common" doesn't mean

- It doesn't mean "not serious." Common does not equal mild.
- It doesn't mean "you'll be fine without help." Many people improve
  significantly with treatment, but the natural course without
  treatment is often worse, not better.
- It doesn't mean "everyone has this exactly your way." Symptom
  presentations are personal.

## What it does mean

- The user is not uniquely broken.
- Reaching out for help is a normal action many people take.
- Treatment infrastructure (clinicians, primary care providers,
  crisis lines) exists in part because the population they serve is
  large.

## Where to look for current numbers

NIMH maintains the canonical public-facing summaries at
nimh.nih.gov/health/statistics. The numbers are refreshed as new
surveys publish; this document captures the steady-state framing,
not specific point estimates.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation, not statistics consulting. For
program planning or research, go to the original surveys (NSDUH,
NHIS, NCS-R) and not to summaries.
