# Caring for Your Mental Health — Everyday Habits

Mental health includes emotional, psychological, and social well-being.
It affects how a person thinks, feels, and acts; it also shapes how
they handle stress, relate to others, and make choices. Caring for
mental health is not a one-time fix; it is a set of small, repeatable
habits.

## The basics that consistently matter

These are not exciting. They are the things that show up in every
serious review of what supports mental well-being.

- **Sleep.** Aim for seven to nine hours, on a consistent schedule.
  Sleep affects every other system.
- **Movement.** Regular physical activity is one of the most
  well-studied interventions for mood. The amount that helps is less
  than people often expect — short, consistent walks count.
- **Eat regularly.** Skipping meals destabilizes mood and energy. The
  pattern matters more than perfection in any single meal.
- **Stay connected.** Spend time with people who are good for the
  nervous system. Reach out even when it feels hard, especially then.
- **Limit alcohol and other substances.** Both can mask low mood
  short-term and worsen it medium-term.
- **Make time for things that bring meaning.** Hobbies, creative work,
  service, faith, time outdoors — whatever fits the person. "Pointless"
  enjoyment is not pointless.

## Practices that help in moments of stress

- **Slow the breath.** A few minutes of slow, paced breathing can
  bring the nervous system out of a high-alert state.
- **Name what is happening.** Saying or writing "I am anxious about X"
  is more useful than running from the feeling.
- **Step away from screens.** Especially news and social media, when
  they are the trigger.
- **Move.** A walk often does what willpower will not.

## When to ask for more support

Self-care is not a substitute for professional care when symptoms are
severe, persistent, or interfering with daily life. Reasonable
thresholds to reach out to a health care provider:

- Symptoms have lasted more than two weeks.
- Sleep, appetite, work, or relationships are clearly affected.
- The person has lost interest in things that used to feel meaningful.
- Substance use has increased.
- The person is having thoughts of self-harm or suicide — this is an
  emergency, not a "wait and see."

In the United States, the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline is available
24 hours a day by call or text for crisis support.

## Important note

This is general guidance. It is not a substitute for professional
diagnosis or treatment. Mental health care is a normal, common form of
health care.
