# Supporting a Friend or Family Member

When someone in your life is going through a mental health
struggle, "what do I do" is one of the hardest questions. Most
people overestimate how clever they have to be and underestimate
how much regular contact matters. The basics, done consistently, are
the high-leverage move.

## What helps

- **Show up.** A short check-in beats a long absence. "Thinking of
  you" texts count.
- **Listen.** Without fixing. Without rushing to advice. Without
  steering toward "but have you tried...". People mostly want to
  feel heard, not solved.
- **Take it seriously.** Don't minimize ("everyone gets sad",
  "you'll bounce back"). The person may already be working hard not
  to feel like a burden; minimizing reinforces that fear.
- **Ask what kind of support they want.** "Do you want me to listen,
  or do you want me to help you figure something out?" gives the
  person the steering wheel.
- **Help with logistics.** Driving them to a first appointment,
  sitting with them while they make a call, helping research
  options — these are often the friction that keeps people stuck.
- **Stay over time.** Mental health struggles do not resolve on a
  social-media-attention cycle. The friend who shows up in week six
  matters more than the cluster of people who showed up in week
  one.

## What to avoid

- "Everything happens for a reason."
- "Try yoga / a hike / cold plunges / clean eating." (Lifestyle is
  legitimate; presented as the answer to clinical-level distress, it
  reads as dismissal.)
- "You should be over this by now."
- "Other people have it worse."
- Comparing their struggle to your worst day. Misery contests do not
  help.

## When to take it more seriously

- If the person talks about feeling like a burden, wanting to
  disappear, or having no reason to live — that is not "venting."
  Ask directly: "Are you thinking about suicide?"
- If the person is no longer doing basics (eating, sleeping,
  bathing, getting out of the house) for more than a few days, that
  is a marker.
- If you are scared, trust that. Mention it gently: "I am worried.
  Can we get you connected with someone?"

## Taking care of yourself

Supporting someone is real work. You also need a person to talk to
about what it is like to be on this side of the situation. Not
necessarily a clinician — sometimes a friend or family member of
your own, sometimes a support group for caregivers or for families
of people with the same condition.

## When the situation is an emergency

If a person is in immediate danger of harming themselves or
someone else, that is an emergency. In the US, 988 (call or text)
is the suicide and crisis line; 911 is appropriate when immediate
medical or safety risk is present. Trained dispatchers can help
decide what response makes sense.

## Important note

You are not their therapist, and you do not have to be. Connecting
them to one is enough.
