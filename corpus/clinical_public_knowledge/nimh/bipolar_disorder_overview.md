# Bipolar Disorder — Overview

Bipolar disorder is a mental health condition that causes unusual and
intense shifts in mood, energy, activity levels, and concentration.
These shifts can make it hard to carry out day-to-day tasks. The
condition is treatable, and most people with bipolar disorder can have
healthy, productive lives with the right care.

## Two ends of the mood shift

People with bipolar disorder experience episodes of unusually elevated
mood (called mania or hypomania, depending on severity) and episodes
of depression. Between episodes, mood may be stable.

### Manic or hypomanic episodes can include

- Feeling very "up," "high," elated, or unusually irritable.
- Feeling jumpy or wired; having more energy than usual.
- Decreased need for sleep — the person feels rested on much less
  sleep than they would normally need.
- Talking very fast about many different things.
- Racing thoughts.
- Doing risky things — spending sprees, impulsive decisions, risky
  sexual behavior, reckless driving.
- Feeling unusually important, talented, or powerful.

Mania is a severe form that can interfere with daily life and may
require hospital care. Hypomania is a less severe form.

### Depressive episodes can include

- Feeling very sad, hopeless, or empty.
- Loss of interest in almost all activities.
- Feeling slowed down or restless.
- Difficulty sleeping; sleeping too much; waking too early.
- Eating too little or too much.
- Difficulty concentrating; forgetting things.
- Thoughts of death or suicide.

## When to seek help

If a person has noticed episodes of unusually high energy or mood paired
with episodes of low energy or mood, especially if these episodes
affect work, relationships, sleep, or judgment, it is worth talking to
a health care provider. Bipolar disorder is often misdiagnosed as
depression alone if the manic or hypomanic episodes have not been
mentioned in the conversation. Bringing them up matters.

If a person is having thoughts of suicide or self-harm, that is an
emergency. In the United States, the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline is
available 24/7 by call or text.

## What treatment can look like

Bipolar disorder is typically treated with medication, psychotherapy,
or both, often with mood-stabilizing medication as a core element. A
consistent treatment relationship matters because the condition is
ongoing and the plan is adjusted over time. Sleep, alcohol use, and
substance use have outsized effects on mood stability in bipolar
disorder; they are worth discussing with a provider.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation, not a diagnostic tool. Only a
qualified health professional can diagnose bipolar disorder. Self-
diagnosis from a website is not reliable.
