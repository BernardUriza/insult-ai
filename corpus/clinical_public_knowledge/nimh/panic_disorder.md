# Panic Disorder — When Fear Overwhelms

Panic disorder is a condition in which a person experiences sudden,
unexpected episodes of intense fear that come on without an obvious
trigger and peak within minutes. The episodes are called panic
attacks. A single panic attack does not mean a person has panic
disorder; the diagnosis requires repeated unexpected attacks plus
persistent worry about more attacks or about what the attacks mean.

## What a panic attack looks and feels like

Panic attacks come on quickly and usually peak within ten minutes.
Symptoms can include:

- Racing heart, pounding heartbeat.
- Sweating, trembling, shaking.
- Sensations of shortness of breath or being smothered.
- Chest pain or discomfort.
- Nausea or stomach distress.
- Dizziness, lightheadedness, or feeling faint.
- Numbness or tingling.
- Hot flashes or chills.
- A sense of unreality or being detached from oneself.
- Fear of losing control, "going crazy," or dying.

The physical symptoms can be intense enough that a first panic attack
is sometimes mistaken for a heart attack. Many people end up in an
emergency room before learning what they actually experienced.

## When to seek help

After a first panic attack, talking to a primary care provider is
reasonable both to rule out medical causes (some thyroid and cardiac
conditions produce similar symptoms) and to discuss next steps if the
attacks repeat. Panic disorder is the right framework when the
attacks recur and produce significant worry between them or
avoidance of places where attacks have happened.

## What treatment can look like

Panic disorder is highly treatable. The two main approaches are
psychotherapy — particularly cognitive behavioral therapy that
includes interoceptive exposure (gradual practice with the physical
sensations of panic in a controlled setting) — and medication, often
SSRIs or SNRIs. Many people respond to one approach; others benefit
from a combination. Caffeine reduction often helps as a complement.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation, not a diagnostic tool. Panic disorder
is diagnosed by qualified clinicians.
