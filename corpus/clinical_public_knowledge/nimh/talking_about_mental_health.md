# Talking About Mental Health — How to Start

Talking about mental health is one of the strongest things a person
can do for their own well-being and for the people around them.
Silence keeps people stuck; conversation usually does not.

This document is for two situations:

1. A user who wants to bring up their own mental health with a
   friend, family member, or partner.
2. A user who wants to bring it up with a primary care provider or
   a clinician.

Both situations come up in Roast Coach sessions ("how do I tell my
partner I'm not okay?", "what do I even say to a doctor?").

## Talking to someone in your life

- **Pick a moment with space.** Not in passing, not over text alone,
  not during a fight.
- **Use specific language.** "I've been sleeping badly for a month
  and snapping at people" beats "I'm not okay."
- **Say what kind of response you want.** "I just want you to know,"
  or "I want help figuring out next steps," or "I want you to come
  with me to an appointment." People often want to help and don't
  know how.
- **Don't expect a perfect response.** The person may freeze or
  default to platitudes. That doesn't mean they don't care; it
  means they're a person, too.
- **Repeat the conversation later if needed.** First conversations
  often do not finish the job.

## Talking to a clinician

- **Bring notes.** Write down what is happening, for how long, what
  you have noticed about triggers, and what you have tried. A short
  list beats trying to remember in the room.
- **Lead with the impact.** "It's affecting my sleep, my work, and
  my relationship" tells a clinician what they need to know in one
  sentence.
- **Mention everything relevant.** Substance use, sleep, eating,
  relationships, medical history, family history. Things that seem
  unrelated often turn out to be relevant.
- **Ask the questions.** "What do you think is going on?", "What
  are the treatment options?", "What's the plan for the next four
  weeks?", "What should I watch for that would mean we adjust?"
- **It is fine to say "I don't fit with you."** A clinician you do
  not connect with is not the right clinician, and looking for a
  better fit is a normal thing to do.

## What if the response is bad

Sometimes the response from a person in your life — or even a
clinician — is dismissive, minimizing, or hurtful. That is the other
person's limitation, not evidence that you are wrong to have spoken.
A bad response is a reason to find a different ear, not to go
silent.

## Important note

If a person is in crisis or having thoughts of suicide, the
conversation is not a casual one. In the US, the 988 Suicide and
Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988) is staffed 24/7 by trained
counselors, and they will help with what to say next.
