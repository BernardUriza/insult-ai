# Self-Harm — Awareness and Help

Self-harm is hurting oneself on purpose, usually as a way to cope with
intense emotional pain. The most common forms include cutting,
burning, scratching, hitting oneself, or interfering with wound
healing. Self-harm is not the same as a suicide attempt, but it is a
serious sign that a person is struggling and needs support. People who
self-harm are at higher risk for suicidal thoughts and attempts.

## Common signs (in self or others)

- Cuts, burns, scars, or bruises in patterns suggesting they are
  self-inflicted (often in places easy to cover with clothing).
- Wearing long sleeves or pants in weather that does not call for them.
- Reluctance or refusal to undress for medical or sports settings.
- Carrying sharp objects or having them in unusual places.
- Withdrawing socially around the times harm is occurring.
- Expressions of self-hatred or unbearable emotional pain.

## What helps

- **Listen without panicking.** A calm response is more useful than a
  shocked one. The person already feels shame.
- **Ask directly.** "Are you hurting yourself? Are you thinking about
  hurting yourself worse?" Asking does not increase risk.
- **Validate the pain, not the method.** The pain is real; the harm
  is a coping strategy that has costs.
- **Help them connect with a professional.** This is the move that
  actually helps over time.
- **Stay connected.** Isolation worsens the pattern.

## When to escalate

- If a person is having thoughts of suicide or has injured themselves
  in a way that requires medical attention, the situation is an
  emergency. In the US, the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline is
  available 24/7 by call or text; 911 or the nearest emergency room
  is appropriate for immediate medical risk.
- If a person describes increasing frequency, severity, or a plan,
  that is a reason to involve a professional sooner rather than later.

## Treatment

Self-harm responds to psychotherapy, especially approaches that
teach distress tolerance and emotional regulation (DBT and CBT
variants). Underlying conditions — depression, anxiety, trauma,
eating disorders — are addressed alongside. Medication may have a
role for co-occurring conditions.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation, not a diagnostic tool or a crisis
service. If you or someone you know is in crisis, please use the
crisis resources above.
