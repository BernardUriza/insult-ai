# Substance Use and Mental Health

Substance use disorders and mental health conditions often occur
together. The combination is sometimes called co-occurring disorders
or dual diagnosis. Many people with mental health conditions use
alcohol or drugs to cope with symptoms; over time, the substance use
can become its own problem, and it usually worsens the underlying
mental health condition.

This is not a character issue. Substance use disorders are recognized
medical conditions with effective treatments.

## How the two relate

The relationship runs both ways and is complicated:

- **Self-medication.** A person with anxiety may use alcohol to
  reduce immediate symptoms; the alcohol then disrupts sleep and
  raises baseline anxiety.
- **Substance-induced symptoms.** Heavy use of alcohol, cannabis,
  stimulants, or other substances can produce symptoms that look like
  depression, anxiety, or psychosis. These sometimes resolve with
  cessation; sometimes they don't.
- **Shared vulnerabilities.** Genetic, developmental, and
  environmental factors can predispose a person to both.

The clinical implication is that treating one without the other
tends to underperform. Treating both together — at the same time, by
the same team or coordinated teams — tends to work better.

## Common signs that use has become a problem

- Using more, or for longer, than intended.
- Repeated attempts to cut down or stop without success.
- A great deal of time spent obtaining, using, or recovering from
  the substance.
- Strong cravings.
- Use that interferes with major roles at work, school, or home.
- Continued use despite social or interpersonal problems caused or
  worsened by it.
- Giving up important activities to use.
- Use in situations that are physically hazardous.
- Tolerance (needing more to get the same effect) or withdrawal.

## When to seek help

There is no "rock bottom" threshold a person has to wait for. Reaching
out earlier produces better outcomes. A primary care provider is a
reasonable starting point and can refer to substance-use specialists.
In the US, the SAMHSA National Helpline (1-800-662-HELP) is free,
confidential, and 24/7.

## What treatment can look like

Treatment varies by substance, severity, and the person, and can
include therapy (motivational interviewing, CBT, contingency
management), medication for some substances (alcohol, opioids,
tobacco), peer support and recovery programs, and treatment of the
co-occurring mental health condition.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation. Diagnosis and treatment of substance
use disorders require qualified clinicians.
