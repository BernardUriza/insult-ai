# Anxiety Disorders — Overview

Occasional anxiety is a normal part of life — worrying about a job
interview, a test, or a difficult decision is expected. Anxiety disorders
are different. People with an anxiety disorder have intense, excessive,
and persistent worry and fear about everyday situations. These feelings
often interfere with daily activities, are difficult to control, and are
out of proportion to the actual danger.

## Common types

- **Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD):** persistent and excessive worry
  about a number of different things — work, health, family, finances —
  that lasts six months or longer.
- **Panic disorder:** recurrent panic attacks (sudden episodes of intense
  fear or discomfort that peak within minutes) plus worry about having
  more attacks or about what the attacks mean.
- **Social anxiety disorder:** intense fear of being watched and judged
  by others, severe enough to interfere with school, work, or daily
  activities.
- **Phobia-related disorders:** intense fear of specific objects or
  situations (heights, flying, animals) that is out of proportion to the
  actual risk.

## Common signs and symptoms

Symptoms vary by disorder and by person, but common ones include:

- Feeling restless, wound-up, or on edge.
- Being easily fatigued.
- Difficulty concentrating; mind going blank.
- Irritability.
- Muscle tension.
- Sleep problems — difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or
  restless, unsatisfying sleep.
- A sense of impending danger, panic, or doom.
- Rapid heart rate, sweating, trembling, or shortness of breath during
  acute episodes.

## When to seek help

A person should consider talking to a health care provider when worry,
fear, or panic feels out of proportion to the situation, lasts longer
than would be expected, and gets in the way of normal life — work,
relationships, sleep, eating. A primary care provider is a reasonable
starting point and can refer onward.

## What treatment can look like

Anxiety disorders are highly treatable. The main approaches are
psychotherapy (cognitive behavioral therapy is especially well-studied),
medication, or both. Many people also benefit from stress-management
techniques and lifestyle adjustments — regular movement, consistent
sleep, reducing caffeine and alcohol, mindfulness practice — as a
complement to clinical care. Treatment tends to work better when started
sooner rather than later, but it is not too late at any point.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation, not a diagnostic tool. Only a qualified
health professional can diagnose an anxiety disorder.
