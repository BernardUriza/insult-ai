# Finding Mental Health Help

Knowing where to start when you decide to look for help is itself a
common stuck point. This document is the practical roadmap, US
context.

## Starting points

- **Primary care provider.** Often the easiest entry point. They can
  evaluate, treat common conditions, refer to specialists, and
  prescribe many psychiatric medications.
- **Insurance directory.** Most US plans have a searchable directory
  of in-network mental health providers. Call the customer service
  number on the card if the online directory is confusing.
- **Employee Assistance Program (EAP).** If your employer has one,
  it typically offers a small number of free sessions and a referral
  network. Confidential.
- **University counseling center.** For students, often free or
  low-cost, on campus.
- **Community mental health centers.** Sliding-scale fees, public
  funding. Search "community mental health" plus your city.

## What to look for in a provider

- **Credentials.** Psychiatrist (MD/DO) and psychiatric nurse
  practitioner can prescribe; psychologist (PhD/PsyD) generally
  cannot; licensed clinical social worker (LCSW), licensed
  professional counselor (LPC), licensed marriage and family
  therapist (LMFT) are common therapy providers.
- **Specialty.** Some clinicians focus on a condition (OCD, eating
  disorders, trauma) or population (adolescents, older adults). For
  conditions with specialized treatments, a specialist usually beats
  a generalist.
- **Modalities offered.** CBT, DBT, EMDR, ERP, IPT, ACT — if a
  specific approach is recommended for your condition, ask whether
  the clinician does it.
- **Logistics.** Hours, location or telehealth, insurance, fees,
  waitlist length.

## Tele-mental health

Telehealth for mental health is widely available and has good
evidence for many conditions. For people with transportation,
schedule, or geographic limitations, it is often the realistic path.

## Free / low-cost options

- **Open Path Psychotherapy Collective** — sliding-scale therapy.
- **NAMI Helpline** — 1-800-950-NAMI (NAMI Helpline; not a crisis
  line) for navigation and resources.
- **Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs)** — community-based,
  sliding-scale.
- **Crisis Text Line** — text HOME to 741741.

## Crisis resources (US)

- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline** — call or text 988, 24/7.
- **Veterans Crisis Line** — 988, then press 1; or text 838255.
- **In immediate danger** — 911 or the nearest emergency room.

## Outside the US

Most countries have national mental health helplines and public
mental health services. The International Association for Suicide
Prevention maintains a list of crisis lines by country
(iasp.info/resources/Crisis_Centres/).

## Important note

This is general guidance, not personalized advice. Finding the right
support sometimes takes a few tries; the right next step is usually
the most accessible one.
