# Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder — Overview

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a condition in which a person
has uncontrollable, recurring thoughts (obsessions) and repetitive
behaviors (compulsions) they feel driven to perform. The behaviors
are not enjoyable; they are a (usually short-lived) way to reduce the
anxiety the obsessions create. OCD can take a significant amount of
time each day and interfere with relationships, work, and school.

## Obsessions

Obsessions are repeated, unwanted thoughts, urges, or mental images
that cause distress. Common themes include:

- Fear of contamination.
- Aggressive thoughts toward oneself or others that feel "out of
  character."
- Unwanted intrusive thoughts about sex, religion, harm.
- A need for symmetry or order.
- Persistent doubt — "did I lock the door, turn off the stove?"

The person experiencing the obsessions usually recognizes them as
irrational, which adds to the distress.

## Compulsions

Compulsions are behaviors driven by the obsessions, performed to
reduce anxiety or prevent a feared outcome. Examples:

- Excessive hand-washing, cleaning, or showering.
- Repeated checking (locks, appliances, work).
- Mental rituals — silently repeating phrases, counting.
- Arranging items in a precise order.
- Seeking reassurance from others repeatedly about the same concern.

The relief from a compulsion is temporary, which is why the cycle
repeats.

## When to seek help

OCD is treatable, and people do not have to wait until symptoms are
severe to reach out. Reasonable thresholds: obsessions or compulsions
that take more than an hour a day, that interfere with work or
relationships, or that produce significant distress.

## What treatment can look like

The two best-studied approaches are a specific form of psychotherapy
called exposure and response prevention (ERP) — a CBT variant tailored
to OCD — and medication (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors).
Many people benefit from a combination. General talk therapy without
the exposure component tends to underperform for OCD specifically.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation, not a diagnostic tool. OCD is
diagnosed by qualified clinicians.
