# Mental Health Medications — Overview

Medications are one of the main treatment approaches for many
mental health conditions, often used together with psychotherapy.
They do not cure conditions; they manage symptoms, sometimes for a
defined period, sometimes long term. A medication that works is one
that meaningfully reduces symptoms with side effects the person can
live with.

The decision to start, switch, or stop medication is made with a
prescribing clinician (primary care provider, psychiatrist, or
psychiatric nurse practitioner). This document is general education,
not personal advice.

## Common classes

- **Antidepressants:** treat depression and many anxiety disorders.
  SSRIs and SNRIs are the most common first-line choices.
- **Anti-anxiety medications:** benzodiazepines for short-term acute
  use; buspirone for some chronic anxiety; SSRIs and SNRIs as
  long-term options.
- **Mood stabilizers:** for bipolar disorder. Lithium and several
  anticonvulsants are used.
- **Antipsychotics:** for psychosis, bipolar disorder, and some
  other conditions.
- **Stimulants and non-stimulants for ADHD:** different classes,
  with different trade-offs.

## What to know before starting

- Most psychiatric medications take time to reach full effect — often
  weeks. Stopping after a few days because "it didn't work" is
  premature.
- Side effects are common in the first one to two weeks and often
  subside; severe or persistent side effects warrant a call to the
  prescriber.
- Some medications interact with alcohol, caffeine, or other
  medications — checking is part of the conversation.
- Stopping abruptly can cause discontinuation symptoms or, with
  some medications, a relapse. A taper plan is the norm.
- Pregnancy, breastfeeding, and planning to conceive change the
  calculus and require an explicit conversation with a prescriber.

## What to know about adjustments

The first medication tried is not always the final one. Switching,
adding, or adjusting doses is a normal part of treatment, not a
sign of failure. Many conditions respond to the second or third
combination after the first did not work.

## Resources

- The prescribing clinician.
- The pharmacist (often underused; pharmacists are an excellent
  resource for side-effect and interaction questions).
- For urgent medication concerns outside of clinic hours, the
  pharmacy or the prescriber's after-hours line.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation. Starting, switching, or stopping
medication is a conversation with a prescriber, not a decision to
make from a website.
