# Depression — Overview

Depression (major depressive disorder) is a common but serious mood
disorder. It causes severe symptoms that affect how a person feels, thinks,
and handles daily activities such as sleeping, eating, and working. To be
diagnosed with depression, the symptoms must be present for at least two
weeks. Depression is more than feeling sad for a few days or having a
rough week.

## Common signs and symptoms

People living with depression do not all experience the same symptoms. The
severity, frequency, and duration of symptoms vary by person and by the
particular illness. Common signs and symptoms include:

- Persistent sad, anxious, or "empty" mood.
- Feelings of hopelessness or pessimism.
- Feelings of irritability, frustration, or restlessness.
- Loss of interest or pleasure in hobbies and activities that once felt
  enjoyable.
- Fatigue, decreased energy, or feeling slowed down.
- Difficulty concentrating, remembering, or making decisions.
- Sleep changes — sleeping too little or too much, waking too early.
- Appetite or weight changes — eating too little or too much.
- Aches or pains, headaches, cramps, or digestive problems without a clear
  physical cause that do not ease with treatment.
- Thoughts of death or suicide, or suicide attempts.

## When to seek help

Depression is treatable. If symptoms have been present nearly every day,
most of the day, for at least two weeks, a person should reach out to a
health care provider. People do not need to wait until symptoms are severe
to get help; early support tends to work better. A primary care provider
can be a starting point and can refer to a mental health specialist when
appropriate.

If a person is having thoughts of suicide or self-harm, that is an
emergency. In the United States, the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline is
available by call or text, 24 hours a day.

## What treatment can look like

Depression is treated with talk therapy (psychotherapy), medication, or a
combination. The right approach depends on the person, their symptoms,
their history, and their preferences. Lifestyle factors — sleep, regular
movement, social connection, reducing alcohol use — also matter and can
support whatever treatment is in place. Many people improve significantly
once treatment is in place; the first plan tried is not always the final
one, and adjusting the plan is normal.

## Important note

This information is general psychoeducation. It is not a diagnostic tool.
Only a qualified health professional can diagnose depression.
