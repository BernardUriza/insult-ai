# Social Anxiety Disorder — More Than Just Shyness

Social anxiety disorder is an intense, persistent fear of being
watched and judged by others. The fear can affect work, school, and
other daily activities. People with social anxiety disorder may avoid
or struggle through situations that put them in the spotlight, like
giving a talk, meeting new people, or even eating in public.

This is not the same thing as introversion or ordinary shyness. Most
people feel nervous before a speech; social anxiety is more severe,
more pervasive, and meaningfully interferes with daily life.

## Common signs

- Intense anxiety in situations involving other people.
- Anxiety that is out of proportion to the actual social risk.
- Physical symptoms — blushing, sweating, trembling, racing heart,
  nausea, mind going blank.
- Avoidance of social situations, or enduring them with extreme
  distress.
- Worry that lasts days or weeks before an anticipated social event.
- Worry afterward about how one came across.

## When to consider getting help

If the anxiety has lasted six months or longer, interferes with work
or relationships, or is paired with significant avoidance (turning
down opportunities, missing meetings, declining invitations because of
the dread), it is worth talking to a health care provider. Many
people delay because they assume "I'm just shy"; the criteria above
are what distinguish ordinary shyness from a treatable condition.

## What treatment can look like

Social anxiety disorder is highly treatable. The two main approaches
are psychotherapy (cognitive behavioral therapy, often with
incremental exposure to feared social situations) and medication.
Some people respond well to one, others to a combination. Treatment
usually starts with what is most feasible and adjusts from there.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation, not a diagnostic tool. Social anxiety
disorder is diagnosed by qualified clinicians.
