# Borderline Personality Disorder — Overview

Borderline personality disorder (BPD) is a mental health condition
marked by ongoing patterns of varying moods, self-image, and
behavior. These patterns often result in impulsive actions and
problems in relationships. People with BPD may experience intense
episodes of anger, depression, and anxiety that can last from a few
hours to days.

BPD is treatable. With effective treatment, many people with BPD
experience meaningful symptom reduction over time.

## Common signs

- Frantic efforts to avoid real or imagined abandonment.
- A pattern of intense, unstable relationships, often shifting between
  idealizing and devaluing the other person.
- An unstable sense of self.
- Impulsive behavior in areas that are potentially harmful (spending,
  sex, substance use, reckless driving, binge eating).
- Recurring suicidal behavior, gestures, or self-harm.
- Emotional instability — intense feelings that come and go in hours.
- Chronic feelings of emptiness.
- Inappropriate, intense anger or difficulty controlling anger.
- Transient stress-related paranoia or dissociation.

## When to seek help

If a person notices several of these patterns persisting over time
and across relationships, especially if paired with self-harm or
suicidal thoughts, talking to a mental health professional is
warranted. BPD is sometimes misdiagnosed as bipolar disorder or
depression alone; an evaluation by someone trained in personality
disorders helps sort that out.

If a person is in crisis or having thoughts of suicide, that is an
emergency. In the United States, the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline
is available 24/7 by call or text.

## What treatment can look like

The most studied treatments for BPD are specialized psychotherapies:

- **Dialectical behavior therapy (DBT):** combines acceptance with
  change strategies; teaches mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion
  regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness.
- **Mentalization-based therapy.**
- **Schema therapy.**
- **Transference-focused psychotherapy.**

Medication is not a primary treatment for BPD itself but may help with
co-occurring conditions (depression, anxiety).

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation, not a diagnostic tool. BPD is
diagnosed by qualified clinicians. Self-diagnosis from online
checklists is not reliable; the patterns overlap with other
conditions in ways that require trained evaluation.
