# Sleep and Mental Health

Sleep and mental health are closely linked. Poor sleep can worsen
anxiety, low mood, and stress; mental health conditions can in turn
disrupt sleep. The relationship goes both ways, which means working on
either side tends to help the other.

## What good sleep looks like

Most adults need seven to nine hours of sleep per night. "Enough sleep"
is not just total hours — it also includes:

- Falling asleep within a reasonable window (about 15 to 30 minutes).
- Staying asleep through the night with at most one or two brief
  awakenings.
- Waking up feeling rested most days.
- A roughly consistent sleep and wake time, including on weekends.

Sleep that is too long, too short, too fragmented, or whose timing
shifts dramatically day to day is worth attention.

## Common patterns to notice

- Difficulty falling asleep because the mind is "running."
- Waking in the early hours and not being able to return to sleep.
- Sleeping a great deal but waking unrested.
- Daytime fatigue, irritability, or difficulty concentrating that
  follows a stretch of poor sleep.
- A pattern of using screens late into the night, then sleeping later
  and feeling worse the next day.

## Habits that protect sleep

These are not cures, but they reduce the friction between a person and
sleep.

- **Consistent timing.** Going to bed and waking at similar times each
  day stabilizes the internal clock.
- **Reduce stimulants late in the day.** Caffeine has a long half-life;
  an afternoon coffee can still be active at bedtime.
- **Watch alcohol.** Alcohol can help with falling asleep but
  fragments sleep later in the night.
- **Wind down deliberately.** A simple low-light, low-stimulation
  half hour before bed signals the body that sleep is approaching.
- **Move during the day.** Regular physical activity supports deeper
  sleep.
- **Keep the bedroom for sleep.** Working, scrolling, and watching long
  videos in bed weakens the association between bed and sleep.

## When to seek help

If sleep problems last longer than a few weeks, if a person is
exhausted despite enough hours in bed, if loud snoring is paired with
choking or gasping, or if sleep problems are paired with persistent low
mood or anxiety, it is worth talking to a health care provider. Sleep
problems can be a symptom of treatable conditions — sleep apnea,
depression, anxiety, thyroid issues — and sorting that out is what
professionals are for.

## Important note

This is general guidance, not a diagnostic tool. Persistent sleep
problems deserve a professional opinion.
