# Attention-Deficit / Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) — Overview

ADHD is a neurodevelopmental condition marked by an ongoing pattern of
inattention, hyperactivity, impulsivity, or some combination, severe
enough to interfere with daily functioning. It is not a moral failing,
a discipline problem, or evidence of low intelligence. It is a
condition that responds to treatment and to environmental adjustments.

## Common signs

ADHD presents in three rough patterns. A given person may move between
them, especially with age.

- **Inattentive:** difficulty sustaining attention on tasks that are not
  novel or urgent, frequent forgetfulness, losing track of items needed
  to complete a task, trouble organizing work, avoiding tasks that
  require sustained mental effort, easily sidetracked.
- **Hyperactive-impulsive:** restlessness, fidgeting, talking
  excessively, interrupting, difficulty waiting, acting before thinking,
  a sense of being "driven by a motor."
- **Combined:** features of both.

In adults the hyperactivity often shifts to internal restlessness,
chronic difficulty with planning and follow-through, and a sense of
"working hard to look normal."

## When to consider getting evaluated

Some forgetfulness or restlessness is normal. ADHD is diagnosed when
the pattern has been present since childhood (even if undiagnosed),
shows up across more than one setting (work and home, or school and
relationships), and interferes with daily life. Common reasons people
seek evaluation as adults include repeated job difficulties, chronic
lateness despite effort, a child being diagnosed and recognizing
themselves in the description, or persistent shame about productivity.

## What treatment can look like

ADHD is treatable. Approaches include behavioral strategies (external
structure, accountability, environmental design), psychotherapy
(especially CBT focused on planning and emotional regulation),
medication (stimulants and non-stimulants both used), and often a
combination. Sleep, exercise, and reducing alcohol use have outsized
effects in ADHD; treatment that ignores them tends to underperform.

## Important note

This is general psychoeducation. ADHD is diagnosed by qualified
clinicians using structured criteria, not by online checklists.
